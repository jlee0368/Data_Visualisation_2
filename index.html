What are they? üì°


MyRTKnet is Malaysia's nationwide Real-Time Kinematic GNSS reference station network, maintained by the Department of Survey and Mapping Malaysia (JUPEM), with a goal to provide real-time GNSS corrections.


How it works? üõ∞Ô∏è


  • Permanent reference stations are distributed across Malaysia
  • These stations stream GNSS observations continuously to a central control center at JUPEM
  • The control center processes corrections and generates network correction streams
  • Users connect via internet/data link to receive these corrections real time


Network Overview ‚Äî Stations & Major Cities

The MyRTKnet reference stations are strategically distributed across both Peninsular Malaysia and East Malaysia (Sabah and Sarawak). Every major region is covered, confirming that JUPEM's network design ensures consistent nationwide GNSS correction services. Coverage density is higher along the west coast corridor of Peninsular Malaysia, aligning with population and economic centers like Klang Valley, Penang and Johor Bahru.


How Remote Are Stations? ‚Äî Distance to Nearest City


Note: This chart covers only the 25th percentile (Q1), Median (marked as blue circle) and 75th percentile(Q3).


The chart reveals clear geographic disparities in station remoteness across Malaysia. Sabah and Sarawak stand out with median distances exceeding 150 km, and wide Q1-Q3 ranges, meaning many stations there are far from urban centers. In contrast, states in Peninsular Malaysia have much smaller distances (typically below 50 km), showing that their reference stations are well integrated into city networks. Melaka has the smallest median distance, possibly due to Melaka being a small state.


Where Are Stations located? ‚Äî A Closer Look



Most MyRTKnet stations lie close to urban centers, shown by the dense clusters of lighter points across both Peninsular and East Malaysia. In contrast, darker point in the interiors of Sabah and Sarawak indicate stations located far from major cities, which are essential for extending national coverage across remote, mountainous regions. This distribution highlights how the network balances accessibility in populated lowlands with strategic installations in rural terrain.


Terrain & Siting Elevation


The histogram shows a strong skew toward low-lying elevations. Over 80% of stations are sited below 200 m, reflecting the concentration of Malaysia's urban and coastal areas. Only a handful of stations exceed 600 m, representing those positioned on upland or interior terrain (notably in Pahang and Sabah).


Sabah and Pahang exhibit the widest elevation ranges, with stations extending above 1,000 m, indicative of deployment across mountainous terrain. Most west-coast states cluster tightly below 100 m, emphasizing accessibility and ease of maintenance.

Receiver Model


The network is dominated by two receiver families: TRIMBLE NETR5 and LEICA GR25. The other two appear in small numbers, which are likely newer point upgrades or site-specific replacements. Operationally, this means we only need to track two primary firmware and spare-parts (Trimble vs Leica).


Antenna Model


The network consists of only two types of antenna which are of almost equal usage across the stations.


Toward a Resilient MyRTKnet


Building a resilient MyRTKnet involves not only expanding coverage but also ensuring real-time fault monitoring and interoperability with next-generation GNSS constellations.


Continued investment in site upgrades, power reliability, and data-sharing infrastructure will ensure the network supports precise positioning disaster resilience, and smart nation initiative for years to come.
